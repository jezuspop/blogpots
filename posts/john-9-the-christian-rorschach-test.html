Bible Study July 14, 2026

The Christian Rorschach Test

One miracle. Four different reactions. What people saw in Jesus revealed what they were already carrying inside.

That You May Believe: The Writings of John

  • Big Idea: Is our perception true reality?
  • John 9: One miracle produces four very different responses.
  • John's Irony: The man born blind is the only person who ends up truly seeing.

John 8 closes with a dramatic scene. Jesus declares, "Before Abraham was, I am," and the crowd picks up stones to kill him. He slips away from the temple. John 9 opens immediately afterward: "As he went along, he saw a man blind from birth."

Jesus walks out of a near-execution and straight into a healing. The contrast is intentional. He moves from people who wanted to snuff out the light to a man who had never seen it.

Is Our Perception True Reality?

What if your biggest blind spot is not what you do not know, but what you think you already know?

A Rorschach test asks people to look at the same inkblot. Different people see different images based on what they carry internally: their experiences, assumptions, and expectations. John 9 gives us a theological version of that test.

  • The Disciples: Religiously Conditioned. They cannot see past their tradition.
  • The Neighbors: Skeptically Stuck. They cannot see past their opinions.
  • The Pharisees: Proudly Resistant. They cannot see past their system.
  • The Parents: Fearfully Reactionary. They cannot see past their social status.
1

The Disciples: Religiously Conditioned

The disciples' question was theologically mainstream, not malicious. Their tradition had preloaded it.

"Neither this man nor his parents sinned," said Jesus, "but this happened so that the works of God might be displayed in him."

John 9:1-7 (NIV)

Jesus reframes suffering. This man's blindness is not a verdict on his past. It is an opportunity to see God work. The disciples were willing to be corrected, and that separates them from the Pharisees later in the chapter.

Questions to Consider

  1. Where might your assumptions be causing you to miss what God is showing you?
  2. Are you asking a blame question where Jesus is inviting a purpose question?
2

The Neighbors: Skeptically Stuck

The man had always been "the blind beggar." His condition had become his identity. When that identity disappeared, some neighbors found it easier to believe he was a lookalike than to accept that he could change.

"Some claimed that he was. Others said, 'No, he only looks like him.' But he himself insisted, 'I am the man.'"

John 9:8-12 (NIV)

Familiarity can blind us to the possibility of transformation. The man's answer is the first testimony in the chapter. He is not yet a follower and does not fully know who Jesus is, but he knows what happened to him.

3

The Pharisees: Proudly Resistant

The Pharisees were blinded by pride. Their resistance was not merely intellectual; it was positional. A Galilean carpenter healing on the Sabbath without their sanction threatened the system that made them Israel's authorized spiritual interpreters.

"Some of the Pharisees said, 'This man is not from God, for he does not keep the Sabbath.' But others asked, 'How can a sinner perform such signs?' So they were divided."

John 9:13-18 (NIV)

Not every Pharisee was uniformly hostile. Some asked the right question. Yet institutional pressure allowed the hardliners to prevail. The Sabbath became a filter that let them dismiss the power of God standing in front of them.

The disciples and Pharisees came from the same religious tradition. The difference is what happened to that conditioning over time. The disciples were willing to be shaped by Jesus. The Pharisees had built an entire system of power around their traditions.

Religion that is unwilling to be corrected by Jesus will eventually begin resisting Jesus in the name of religion.

Questions to Consider

  1. Would you rather have truth, or just be right?
  2. Have your convictions remained biblical while your posture has become unteachable?
  3. Are you asking controlling questions, or looking for where God is at work?
4

The Parents: Fearfully Reactionary

Synagogue expulsion could devastate a family. The synagogue was not merely a religious building. It stood at the center of social, economic, and communal life. The parents knew the facts, but chose personal security over public testimony.

"His parents said this because they were afraid of the Jewish leaders, who already had decided that anyone who acknowledged that Jesus was the Messiah would be put out of the synagogue."

John 9:19-23 (NIV)

Fear did not erase their facts. It silenced their faith. The Pharisees are the dramatic villains, but the parents may be the most relatable people in the story. Most of us do not lose our nerve to an intellectual argument. We lose it to social pressure.

The Contrast: The Healed Man

Jesus heard that they had thrown him out, and when he found him, he said, "Do you believe in the Son of Man?"

Then the man said, "Lord, I believe," and he worshiped him.

John 9:35-39 (NIV)

The man born blind is the only person in the chapter who sees clearly. His sight comes at a cost. He loses his community, his parents' public support, and the only religious institution his world had. Yet when the world pushes him out, Jesus does not abandon him. Jesus comes looking for him.

The one who pays the highest social price receives the highest gift: a personal revelation of Jesus' identity and the grace to worship.

How to Clear the Picture

1

Diagnose Your Lens

Ask what is shaping how you read this moment: Scripture, tradition, fear, pride, or pain?

2

Move From Blame to Purpose

When suffering appears, do not start with "Whose fault is this?" Ask how the works of God might be displayed here.

3

Hold Your Beliefs With Humility

Strong convictions are good. Religious pride is dangerous. If Jesus cannot correct us, we are not following him.

4

Share Your Testimony

Like the healed man, you may not be great at defending your faith, but you can share your transformation: "One thing I do know. I was blind, but now I see!"

5

Choose Costly Faith Over Safe Silence

When fear tempts you to stay quiet, choose to stand. Jesus is worth the social cost.

Final Questions

  1. What assumptions might be distorting how you see Jesus?
  2. Where has fear made you silent about what you know is true?
  3. What would it look like to choose truth over comfort this week?

Everyone in John 9 saw the same miracle, but not everyone saw Jesus. Do you see Jesus?

Bible Study July 14, 2026

The Christian Rorschach Test

One miracle. Four different reactions. What people saw in Jesus revealed what they were already carrying inside.

That You May Believe: The Writings of John

  • Big Idea: Is our perception true reality?
  • John 9: One miracle produces four very different responses.
  • John's Irony: The man born blind is the only person who ends up truly seeing.

John 8 closes with Jesus declaring, "Before Abraham was, I am," and a crowd reaching for stones. John 9 opens as he leaves that near-execution and sees a man who has been blind from birth. Jesus moves from people trying to extinguish the Light to a man who has never seen it.

Is Our Perception True Reality?

What if your biggest blind spot is not what you do not know, but what you think you already know?

A Rorschach test gives several people the same image and receives different interpretations. John 9 gives us a theological version: one miracle, four groups, and four reactions shaped by what each group was already carrying.

The Disciples

Religiously conditioned. They cannot see past their tradition.

The Neighbors

Skeptically stuck. They cannot see past their opinions.

The Pharisees

Proudly resistant. They cannot see past their system.

The Parents

Fearfully reactionary. They cannot see past the social cost.

The man who begins the chapter physically blind is the only person who ends up truly seeing, while those with perfect eyesight are exposed as spiritually blind.
1

The Disciples: Religiously Conditioned

As he went along, he saw a man blind from birth. [2] His disciples asked him, “Rabbi, who sinned, this man or his parents, that he was born blind?” [3] “Neither this man nor his parents sinned,” said Jesus, “but this happened so that the works of God might be displayed in him. [4] As long as it is day, we must do the works of him who sent me. Night is coming, when no one can work. [5] While I am in the world, I am the light of the world.” [6] After saying this, he spit on the ground, made some mud with the saliva, and put it on the man’s eyes. [7] “Go,” he told him, “wash in the Pool of Siloam” (this word means “Sent”). So the man went and washed, and came home seeing.

John 9:1-7 (NIV)
Why They Asked

A Question Their Tradition Had Already Answered

The disciples were not being malicious or intellectually lazy. Their question was theologically mainstream. Second Temple Judaism carried a deep assumption, rooted in Deuteronomy 28 and reinforced by voices like Job's counselors, that suffering could be traced directly to sin.

They had been taught since childhood that covenant faithfulness produced blessing and covenant failure produced suffering. A blind man begging by the road fit that grid perfectly, so their tradition pre-loaded the question before they ever met him.

The Disciples

Their conditioning was inherited, and they remained willing to let Jesus correct it.

The Pharisees

They began with the same cultural background but hardened it into a self-protecting institution.

Same starting point, very different destinations. One group was willing to be corrected; the other was not.

Jesus reframes the man's suffering. His blindness is not a verdict on his past; it is an opportunity for the works of God to be displayed. Sin has real consequences, but that truth does not give us permission to read God's mind or rush to judgment. Isaiah 40 reminds us that God's ways are not ours.

The danger of religious tradition is not simply that it can make people bad. It can make us blind while leaving us feeling secure.

Questions to Consider

  1. Where might your assumptions be causing you to miss what God is showing you?
  2. What religious assumptions did your upbringing give you that you have never examined against Scripture?
  3. Are you asking a blame question where Jesus is inviting a purpose question?
2

The Neighbors: Skeptically Stuck

His neighbors and those who had formerly seen him begging asked, “Isn’t this the same man who used to sit and beg?” [9] Some claimed that he was. Others said, “No, he only looks like him.” But he himself insisted, “I am the man.” [10] “How then were your eyes opened?” they asked. [11] He replied, “The man they call Jesus made some mud and put it on my eyes. He told me to go to Siloam and wash. So I went and washed, and then I could see.” [12] “Where is this man?” they asked him. “I don’t know,” he said.

John 9:8-12 (NIV)
Why They Doubted

His Condition Had Become His Identity

The neighbors' doubt was not irrational. Miraculous healings were not daily events, and a man known for years as a blind beggar was suddenly claiming that he could see. In the ancient world, identity was tightly connected to social role and community recognition. To them, he had always been the blind beggar.

He was so familiar that his condition had become his name. When the condition disappeared, some found it easier to believe he was a lookalike than to accept that the man they knew could become something more.

The Power of a Label

Known by the Struggle, Not the Name

Scripture repeatedly introduces people by the condition, reputation, ethnicity, or social role others used to define them:

Samaritan Woman · John 4 The Invalid · John 5 Bleeding Woman · Matthew 9 The Demoniac · Mark 5 The Paralytic · Mark 2 The Leper · Luke 5 The Sinful Woman · Luke 7 The Ethiopian Eunuch · Acts 8 The Canaanite Woman · Matthew 15

This is what happens when we reduce people to their worst selves or chronic struggles. Familiarity can blind us to the possibility of transformation. The neighbors knew his history so well that they could no longer imagine his future.

What He Is Not

He is not yet a follower of Jesus.

What He Does Not Know

He cannot yet fully explain who Jesus is.

What He Knows

“I am the man.” He knows what happened to him.

This is the first testimony in the chapter. He does not have a complete theology yet, but he has an undeniable transformation.
A God Who Transforms

Change Is a Pattern Throughout Scripture

Moses, Gideon, Samson, Naomi, the Samaritan woman, Peter, Martha, and Paul all remind us that God does not freeze people at the worst, weakest, or most familiar chapter of their story. God is in the business of transforming people.

Questions to Consider

  1. Have you quietly started believing certain people cannot change?
  2. Do you live like God can change even the most cynical person?
3

The Pharisees: Proudly Resistant

They brought to the Pharisees the man who had been blind. [14] Now the day on which Jesus had made the mud and opened the man's eyes was a Sabbath. [15] Therefore the Pharisees also asked him how he had received his sight. "He put mud on my eyes," the man replied, "and I washed, and now I see." [16] Some of the Pharisees said, "This man is not from God, for he does not keep the Sabbath." But others asked, "How can a sinner perform such signs?" So they were divided. [17] Then they turned again to the blind man, "What have you to say about him? It was your eyes he opened." The man replied, "He is a prophet." [18] They still did not believe that he had been blind and had received his sight until they sent for the man's parents.

John 9:13–18 (NIV)
The Sabbath Tension

A Real Debate Became a Convenient Filter

Healing on the Sabbath was a genuine interpretive tension in first-century Jewish life. The Torah contained no explicit medical exemption, and actions such as washing, preparing medicine, or anointing a patient could be classified as work.

The deeper question was whether preserving life could override Sabbath restrictions. During the Maccabean Revolt, Mattathias permitted armed defense on the Sabbath after Jews were attacked without resisting, but whether the same priority extended to healing remained unsettled.

An Honest Question Survived

Not every Pharisee was uniformly hostile. Some asked, “How can a sinner perform such signs?” John preserves their division because the evidence was strong enough to disturb the party line.

Institutional Pressure Won

The hardliners prevailed. Majority consensus and institutional pressure silenced those willing to follow the evidence.

Their blindness was not merely intellectual. It was positional. A Galilean carpenter healing on the Sabbath without their approval threatened the system that made them Israel's authorized spiritual interpreters. They understood what was happening, but accepting it would cost them control.

The Disciples

Shared the same religious tradition, but remained willing to be reshaped by Jesus.

The Pharisees

Built a system of power around that tradition and treated correction as a threat.

Religion that is unwilling to be corrected by Jesus will eventually begin resisting Jesus in the name of religion.

The Filter

The Sabbath issue lets them dismiss the power of God in front of them.

The Control Question

Who approved this?

The Kingdom Question

Is God at work here?

Questions to Consider

  1. Would you rather have truth, or simply be right?
  2. Have your convictions remained biblical while your posture has become unteachable?
  3. Are you asking controlling questions, or looking for where God is at work?
4

The Parents: Fearfully Reactionary

"Is this your son?" they asked. "Is this the one you say was born blind? How is it that now he can see?" [20] "We know he is our son," the parents answered, "and we know he was born blind. [21] But how he can see now, or who opened his eyes, we don't know. Ask him. He is of age; he will speak for himself." [22] His parents said this because they were afraid of the Jewish leaders, who already had decided that anyone who acknowledged that Jesus was the Messiah would be put out of the synagogue. [23] That was why his parents said, "He is of age; ask him."

John 9:19–23 (NIV)
The Cost of Expulsion

More Than Losing a Place of Worship

Synagogue expulsion could devastate a family. The synagogue stood at the center of social, economic, and communal life. To be expelled could mean losing a social network, reputation, livelihood, and community identity. For parents whose disabled son had already been treated as a community liability, the threat was real.

They Knew the Facts

They did not disbelieve their son. They knew he was their child, knew he had been born blind, and knew he could now see.

They Chose Security

They weighed the evidence and chose personal safety over public testimony: “He is of age; ask him.”

Fear of social cost is one of Scripture's most underestimated enemies of faith. The Pharisees are the dramatic villains, but the parents are the most relatable people in the story. Most of us do not lose our faith to an intellectual argument. We lose our nerve to social pressure: What will people think? What will this cost me?

Fear did not erase their facts. It silenced their faith.
The Ultimate Tragedy

They Abandoned Their Son

The healed man had no family advocate in his trial. The Pharisees brought him in, interrogated him twice, and threw him out. His parents protected themselves by handing the responsibility back to the son who needed them to stand beside him.

When people are pushed out by the world, Jesus does not abandon them. He pursues them.

The Contrast: The Healed Man

Jesus heard that they had thrown him out, and when he found him, he said, “Do you believe in the Son of Man?” [36] “Who is he, sir?” the man asked. “Tell me so that I may believe in him.” [37] Jesus said, “You have now seen him; in fact, he is the one speaking with you.” [38] Then the man said, “Lord, I believe,” and he worshiped him. [39] Jesus said, “For judgment I have come into this world, so that the blind will see and those who see will become blind.”

John 9:35-39 (NIV)

The Only Clear Picture

The man born blind is the only person in the chapter who learns to see clearly.

“Lord, I believe,” and he worshiped him. (v. 38)

He Lost His Community

The people who had known him longest could not accept what had happened.

He Lost Public Support

His parents confirmed the facts but refused to stand with him.

He Lost His Institution

He was cast out of the only religious community his world had.

Jesus Came Looking

The moment the world pushed him out, Jesus pursued him personally.

The one who paid the highest social price is the one who received the highest gift: the personal revelation of Jesus' full identity, and the grace to worship.

How to Clear the Picture

1. Diagnose Your Lens

Ask what is shaping how you read this moment: Scripture, tradition, fear, pride, or pain?

2. Move From Blame to Purpose

When suffering appears, ask how the works of God might be displayed rather than beginning with whose fault it is.

3. Hold Beliefs With Humility

Strong convictions are good. Religious pride is dangerous. If Jesus cannot correct us, we are not following him.

4. Share Your Testimony

You may not be able to answer every objection, but you can say, “One thing I do know. I was blind, but now I see.”

5. Choose Costly Faith

When fear tempts you toward safe silence, choose to stand. Jesus is worth the social cost.

Final Questions

  1. What assumptions might be distorting how you see Jesus?
  2. Where has fear made you silent about what you know is true?
  3. What would it look like to choose truth over comfort this week?
Everyone in John 9 saw the same miracle, but not everyone saw Jesus. Do you see Jesus?
